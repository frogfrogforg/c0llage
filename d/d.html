on the Exquisite Corpse

there are many variations on the game, so here's the most basic version of the rules for producing the version for two people who are drawing:

  1. The first player draws one half of an object of the choice on one half of a sheet of paper.
  2. Once they have completed their drawing of one half of an object, the first player must fold the piece of paper over so that only a very slim section of their drawing is visible.
  3. Only by looking at this slim section, the second player must take the piece of paper & draw the second half of the object.

the resulting drawing is almost alway seems to be a collaborative non-sequiter, a single image which can't follow itself. the aesthetic discontinuities are pleasing & curious, while the serendipitous continuities become puzzling & exciting in their own right.

the game of exquisite corpse is often creditted to the author of the Surrealist Manifesto, Andre Breton, & it was formalized as a surrealist practice in either the year 1918CE or 1925CE, i can't really find a clear source. the surrealist project overall was an attempt to access more & more pure artifacts of the unconscious mind. like the psychoanalysts who informed their work, the surrealists intrumentalized unintentional, automatic, &/or senseless thoughts to explore the human psyche. i think its important to establish this because the most important part of any drawing produced by an exquisite corpse is its seam, the point where two individuals have met. rather than an attempt to discover a more authentic psychic experience of oneself, the exquisite corpse explores the pleasing inability of separate human subjects to fully integrate with one another.
